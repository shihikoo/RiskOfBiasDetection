a b s t r a c t
Flat-panel volumetric computed tomography (fpVCT) is a non-invasive approach to three-dimensional
small animal imaging. The capability of volumetric scanning and a high resolution in time and space
enables whole organ perfusion studies. We aimed to assess feasibility and validity of fpVCT in cerebral
perfusion measurement with impaired hemodynamics by evaluation of three well-established rat stroke
models for temporary and permanent middle cerebral artery occlusion (MCAO).
Male Wistar rats were randomly assigned to temporary (group I: suture model) and permanent (group
II: suture model; III: macrosphere model) MCAO and to a control group. Perfusion scans with respect to
cerebral blood flow (CBF) and volume (CBV) were performed 24h post intervention by fpVCT, using a
Gantry rotation time of 1s and a total scanning time of 30s. Postmortem analysis included infarct-size
calculation by 2,3,5-triphenyltetrazolium chloride (TTC) staining.
Infarctvolumesdidnotdiffersignificantlythroughoutinterventiongroups.AfterpermanentMCAO,CBF
significantly decreased in subcortical regions to 78.2% (group II, p=0.005) and 79.9% (group III, p=0.012)
andintotalhemisphereto77.4%(groupII,p=0.010)and82.0%(groupIII,p=0.049).CBFwaslessimpaired
with temporary vessel occlusion. CBV measurement revealed no significant differences.
ResultsdemonstratefeasibilityofcerebralperfusionquantificationinratswiththefpVCT,whichcanbe
a useful tool for non-invasive dynamic imaging of cerebral perfusion in rodent stroke models. In addition
to methodological advantages, CBF data confirm the macrosphere model as a useful alternative to the
suture model for permanent experimental MCAO.

1. Introduction
Ischemic stroke can be described as the result from a focal transient or permanent reduction in cerebral blood flow (Dirnagl et al.,
1999). The potential to distinguish the infarct core from the possible salvageable tissue-at-risk can be regarded as one of the central
issues of acute stroke therapy. Since temporal and spatial progress
ofhypoperfusedareaswillstronglyinfluencetherapeuticdecisions,
a thorough and fast investigation of cerebral perfusion is of great
importance.
There are numerous modalities available for perfusion-imaging
in the clinical setting, e.g. positron emission tomography, single
photon emission tomography, magnetic resonance imaging, computed tomography (CT) or Doppler ultrasound (Paciaroni et al.,
2009;Wintermarketal.,2005).CT-perfusionimagingintheclinical
routineispossible,butbeingrestrictedtoseveralconsecutiveslices,
conventional CT-scanners cannot capture hemodynamics of the
whole brain and therefore may not demonstrate and measure full
extend of ischemia (Amar, 2011; Konig, 2003). Recently published
datashowfeasibilityofwhole-braindynamicimaginginhumansby
useofdynamictime-resolvedCT-Angiographyonthebasisofanew
generation 320-detector row 640-slice CT, providing important
hemodynamicinformationbygenerationoftime-resolvedcerebral
angiograms of the whole cerebral vasculature (Menon et al., 2013).
Findings of stroke pathophysiology and evaluation of new therapeutic approaches have largely been derived from experimental
research, providing a broad variety of different animal models for
focal cerebral ischemia (Casals et al., 2011; Gerriets et al., 2004).
Within the meaning of “translational stroke research”, it is worthwhile transferring clinical imaging modalities to small animals in
order to verify models, monitor diseases, and evaluate therapies
(Hirsch, 2003). In order to analyze hemodynamic changes within
this setting, the ideal experimental imaging modality should combine a non-invasive in vivo approach, enabling longitudinal outcomeassessmentwithacceptableresolution(Greschusetal.,2005).
Flat-panel volumetric computed tomography (fpVCT) is a
comparatively new, non-invasive approach to three-dimensional
imaging (Obert et al., 2005). Acquisition of larger volume per rotation with higher resolution than in conventional CT is possible.
Compared to laser Doppler flowmetry, anesthesia can be less deep
andshorterandnotrepanationisrequired.FpVCToffersalargefield
of view and shorter scanning times than micro-CT. High-volume
coverageandcontinuousrotationofthedetectormayenableimaging of dynamic parameters like cerebral perfusion (Greschus et al.,
2005;Guptaetal.,2008).Nevertheless,referringtoimagingoflowcontrast parenchymatic organs like the brain, fpVCT shows a weak
contrast resolution, which affects image quality of these tissues
(Gupta et al., 2008).
The ability to image physiological cerebral blood flow in rats
with high-resolution fpVCT has already been shown (Greschus
et al., 2005). Although offering a promising potential, to our
knowledge, data on the performance and significance of fpVCT
in experimental stroke research has not been published yet. The
present study was conducted to assess feasibility and validity of
cerebral perfusion quantification with impaired hemodynamics by
evaluation of three well established rat stroke models for temporary and permanent middle cerebral artery occlusion (MCAO).
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Animal preparation
All procedures were carried out in accordance with our institutional guidelines and the German animal protection legislation and
were approved by the regional ethics committee.
32 male Wistar Unilever rats (HsdCpb:WU; Harlan Winkelmann
, Borchen, Germany) weighing 290–350g were anesthetized
with 5% isoflurane delivered in air at 3.0L/min for 2min within an
enclosed chamber. Anesthesia was maintained with 2–3% isoflurane delivered in air at 0.5L/min via a facial mask during surgery
while spontaneous breathing was maintained. During the surgical
procedures, body temperature was monitored continuously with a
rectalprobeandmaintainedat36.5–37.0 ◦Cwithathermostatically
controlled water flow system.
The right common (CCA), internal (ICA) and external carotid
artery (ECA) were exposed through a midline incision of the neck.
The ECA was isolated, superior thyroid and occipital arteries were
cauterized. Then the distal portion of the ECA was ligated with a
5-0 suture and transected to create an ECA stump with a length of
approximately 5mm. The pterygopalatine branch of the ICA was
ligated with a 5-0 suture as well.
2.2. Suture models (reperfusion group I, permanent occlusion
group II)
A 4-0 silicone coated nylon suture with a coating length of
10mm (total length 3cm) and a thermically rounded tip (Ethilon,
EthiconGmbH,Norderstedt,Germany)wasintroducedthroughthe
ECA stump as previously described (Gerriets et al., 2003a; Li et al.,
1999). The CCA and ICA were ligated temporarily for this procedure
to prevent hemorrhage. The occluder was slowly advanced into the
ICA 18–22mm beyond the carotid bifurcation until a mild elastic
resistance indicated that the tip was properly lodged in the anterior cerebral artery (ACA), thus blocking blood flow to the middle
cerebral artery (MCA). After the occluder was fixed in place with a
5-0 suture, ligations of the CCA and ICA were removed. Reperfusion
in group I was induced by removing the suture 90min after middle
cerebral artery occlusion (MCAO).
2.3. Macrosphere model (group III)
A PE-50 tubing filled with saline and six TiO2 macrospheres
(0.315–0.355mm in diameter, BRACE GmbH, Germany) was
inserted into the ECA stump. The tip of the tubing was placed in
the carotid bifurcation without affecting the blood flow to the ICA
andfixedwitha5-0suture.Thenmacrosphereswereadvancedseparately into the ICA by a slow injection of approximately 0.05mL
salineeachuntiltheyweremovedpassivelyintothecerebralcirculation by the blood flow. After macrosphere injection, the ICA was
flushed carefully with 0.5mL saline under simultaneous inspection
of the carotid artery. Excessive dilatation of the ICA, indicating high
flushing pressure, was avoided. After that, the tube connections
were removed, followed by a ligation of the ECA stump (Gerriets
et al., 2003a).
2.4. Controls (group IV)
The ECA stump was prepared as described above, but no occlusion was performed.
2.5. Experimental protocol
32 animals were randomly subjected to groups I–IV: group
I, MCAO for 90min, adjacent reperfusion (suture model, n=10);
group II, permanent MCAO (suture model, n=9); group III, permanent MCAO (macrosphere model, n=9), group IV, control group
(n=4).

2.6. Functional testing
Neurological evaluation for all experimental groups was performed at baseline and after 24h, using a previously described
9 point-score for assessment of motor, coordinative and sensory
functions such as contralateral forelimb flexion, hemianopia, sensory to left side touch, instability to lateral push from the right and
coordination when walking on ground. For each clinical deficit, up
to 10 points can be given. The Neuroscore can range from 0 (no
deficit) to 90 (very severe neurological deficit) (Nedelmann et al.,
2007).
Furthermore, motor functions were assessed using the Rotarod-
Test at baseline and after 24h. The wheel was continuously
accelerated from 0 to 30rpm within one minute. The maximum
speed tolerated by the rats without falling off or spinning around
was documented. The difference in Rotarod performance before
and after embolization was calculated (Hamm et al., 1994).
2.7. Radiological investigation
The radiological investigation was performed subsequent to
24h-clinical testing with a flat-panel-based eXplore Locus Ultra
(eLU), a volumetric micro-computer tomography system developed by GE Healthcare, London, Ontario, Canada. The system is
described in detail in Obert et al. (2010).
Inshort,thepeculiarityoftheCTscanneristheuseofaflat-panel
detector for radiation detection. The flat-panel detector consists
of cesium iodide needles and vapour deposited on an amorphous
silicon photosensitive array, which contains a 1024×1024 pixel
matrix. For the perfusion protocol used, 1000 columns and 92
rows are read out from that matrix which enables imaging of
41mm in z-direction. The field of view (FOV) in the xy-plane is
102.4mm×102.4mm. For a total data acquisition time of 30s, 30
time frames of 1s duration were collected. Four hundred sixteen
views were taken in one gantry rotation of 1s duration at 80kVp
and60mA.Thescanparameterswereidenticalforallexaminations
of all specimens.
The experimental setting allowed initiation of anesthesia and
thefollowingimagingproceduredirectlyafter24h-functionaltesting (±45min) in all groups, assuring minimal time delay. The
animals were anesthetized with Ketamine/Xylazine (100mg/kg
Ketamine/5mg/kg Xylazine i.p.). The immobilized animals were
then positioned on the specimen holder of the gantry table prior
to the scan. During the experiment, body temperature of the rats
was measured using a rectal probe (target temperature 37 ◦C) and,
if necessary, controlled with an infrared lamp positioned above
the specimen holder (Fig. 1). This was possible since the specimen
holder drove only shortly into the bore hole of the eLU during the
data-acquisition.
ContrastagentwasusedforfpVCTinvestigationineveryanimal:
0.5mLPeritrastInfusio31%(Dr.F.KöhlerChemieGmbH,Bensheim,
Germany) was injected into the animal’s tail vein as a bolus within
3.5s via syringe driver (ALADDIN, World Precision Instruments,
Sarasota,FL,USA).AsecondsyringedriverwithNaClbuffersolution
was linked to the system by a tee-piece connector constantly flushing the pipes with buffer (100l/min) prior to the contrast bolus
injection and assuring inflow of the contrast agent after its injection. Coordination of this procedure followed a protocol, initiated
by remote control from the workstation outside the scanner room.
This enabled synchronization of the contrast agent injection with
the initialization of the scan. To minimize the length of the tubing,
both syringe drivers were connected to the specimen holder and
could move toward the bore hole during the scan (Fig. 1).
2.8. Image reconstruction
The eLU data sets of the scanned rat heads describe 3d-spatial
coordinates of X-ray attenuation related density values, Hounsfield
units (HU). In perfusion exams the 4th dimension refers to the
time. The data were reconstructed with a cone-beam filtered backprojection algorithm into a [512×512×30voxel] 30 time-frames
matrix. The final 12 bit DICOM image data had an anisotropic voxel
size of 0.2mm2 in the x-, y-plane and 0.9mm in z, which was identical in all exams. The imaged FOV was (102.4mm2) in the xy-plane
and ranged from 27.0 to 32.4mm in the z-plane if 30 or 36 images
in z were chosen during reconstruction. Dependent on the number
of images in z-direction, a total of 900 or up to 1080 images was
obtained for all 30 time frames. A water phantom was positioned
under the rat-bed of the specimen holder, which was necessary to
use a vendor supplied “hysteresis-correction” option that enables
imagehomogeneitycorrections.Tousethe“hysteresis-correction”,
one region of interest (ROI) had to be positioned within the area of
the water-phantom and another ROI had to be positioned in an air
regionwithintheFOV.Theimagereconstructionwasperformedon
aLinux-basedreconstructionclusterasdescribedpreviously(Obert
et al., 2010). After reconstruction, DICOM images were produced
and sent to an Advantage Workstation (AW), where the perfusion
analysis was accomplished.

2.10. Postmortem analysis
After clinical and radiological investigation, the animals were
anesthetized deeply using isoflurane and euthanized by decapitation. The brains were removed and sectioned coronally into 6
slices (thickness each: 2mm), incubated in a 2% solution of 2,3,5-
triphenyltetrazolium chloride (TTC) at 37 ◦C, fixed by immersion in
10%bufferedformalinsolution,andscannedwithacomputerscanner(ScanJet3400C,HewlettPackard;resolution600×600dpi).The
unstained areas of the fixed brain slices were defined as infarcted
area.
With the use of image analysis software (ImageJ; National Institutes of Health), the areas of both hemispheres and of the infarcted
regions were calculated for each slice.
The lesion volume was corrected for the space-occupying
effect of ischemic brain edema using the equation: %HLV=LV/HVI
(%HLV=edema corrected lesion volume, LV=direct lesion volume,
HVI =ipsilateral hemispheric volume).
2.11. Placing the regions of interest (ROI)
Since infarcted brain regions cannot be displayed on perfusion

(a) cortex, (b) subcortex and (c) whole hemisphere (Fig. 4). CBF
and CBV were acquired as mean for each side and in each animal,
thus comparison of data between infarct-hemisphere and noninfarct-hemisphere was possible. Differences in CBF and CBV were

2.12. Statistical analysis
The Shapiro–Wilk-test was used to test for normal distribution
of parametric data. Homogeneity of variance was tested by Levene-
Test. One-Way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) for parametric data
was accomplished. Then the individual groups were subjected to
a pairwise post hoc comparison with Fisher’s Least Significant Difference (LSD) procedure. Data were presented as mean±standard
deviation. The level of probability p<0.05 was regarded as significant.

facilitates whole organ perfusion studies: The feasibility of cerebral
blood flow and cerebral blood volume measurements in healthy
rodents has already been shown in experimental studies (Greschus
et al., 2005).
Quantitative analysis of perfusion is limited by a minimal
gantry-rotation time: It has been shown, that a rotation time of up
to 4s is sufficient for dynamic imaging of brain perfusion in stroke
patients (Wintermark et al., 2004). Gupta et al. investigated suitability of fpVCT in imaging time-dependent vascular pathologies
in small animals and found dynamic processes with a time constant >1.5s to be reliably evaluated. In terms of cerebral perfusion
imaging in the rat, with a comparatively short circulation time at
a heart rate of 300–400bpm, limitations of temporal and contrast
resolution hampered demonstration of differences in transit time
between ischemia affected and control hemisphere in an animal
model of stroke (Gupta et al., 2011). Greschus et al. used a fpVCT
with a minimal gantry-rotation time of two seconds, demonstrating that temporal resolution in this study enabled reliable imaging
of dynamic processes in cerebral vasculature of the rat (Greschus
et al., 2005). Against the background of a strong interdependence
of temporal resolution and gantry-rotation time, we could show
that fpVCT – with a reduced rotation time of 1s and a total scanning time of 30s – can reliably measure perfusion differences in rat
modelsofstroke.However,neitherclinicaltestingnorexamination
of infarct volumes could demonstrate notable differences between
the models used (Figs. 5 and 6).
In our experiment, cerebral perfusion measurement by fpVC
revealed a decrease of cerebral blood flow even 24h after MCAO.
Compared to a control group, differences in ipsilateral subcortex and total ipsilateral hemisphere turned out to be significantly
reduced within groups of permanent MCAO, ranging between 70
and85%ofcontralateralCBF.Reperfusionafter90minshowedonly
slightreductionsinipsilateralsubcorticalandcorticalCBFto90%of
CBF of contralateral hemisphere, being significantly higher as subcortical CBF in permanent suture model of about 78% (p=0.031).
Less impaired perfusion after reperfusion especially in subcortical
structures may be explained by more efficient collateralisation in
this region, even 24h after ischemia onset. Although a delayed
perfusion deficit in subcortical structures after one day is ascertainable
, it seems to be less pronounced than in the first 6–8h
after ischemia onset: Literature with regard to cerebral perfusion
studies in rodents in a time frame of more than just a few hours
is sparse, reflecting the exclusivity of applicable time schedules
of the methodology in the present study. In 1986, Nedergaard
et al. investigated cerebral perfusion after MCAO in rats autoradiographically up to 20h and found a reduction of cortical CBF to
20–30% (Nedergaard et al., 1986). Computer assisted autoradiography was also used by Bolander et al. three years later to detect
perfusion deficits within a time frame of 4 weeks after MCAO in
rats. Here, reduction of 15% in the infarct core and >20% in perifokal
regions, most markedly within first hours post intervention and
followed by subsequent normalization, was described (Bolander
et al., 1989). Both studies documented phases of hyperperfusion
occurring at early and late stages of ischemia between 18h and
7 days after MCAO. In our experimental design including CBF-
measurement once at 24h we were not able to detect phases of
hyperperfusion.

Dependingonstudydesign,experimentaldataonperfusionparameters early after MCAO in rodent models of stroke reveal CBF
reductions to a range of 20–60% from baseline values within the
first hours after vessel occlusion (Buchan et al., 1992; McLeod et al.,
2011; Riva et al., 2012; Walberer et al., 2012). Rodent perfusion
studies after MCAO appear to be heterogeneous with respect to the
strokemodel,imagingdevice,observationalperiod,timepointsand
parameters used. Most of data collected during the last 15 years is
derived from the suture model; present investigation includes the
comparatively novel macrosphere model for experimental stroke
(Gerriets et al., 2003a). Here, compared to the suture model using
nylon suture with a silicon coating length >3.3mm, embolizing
macrospheres do not lead to an ischemic infarction of hypothalamic areas and therefore reduce the risk of reactive hyperthermia
potentially menacing validity of neuroprotective studies (Gerriets
et al., 2003b; Guan et al., 2012). On the other hand, the macrosphere model is not able to simulate reperfusion and always causes
a permanent occlusion of cerebral arteries. Walberer et al. investigated perfusion after macrosphere embolization and observed a
perfusion reduction to 43–76% after 5min and 38–65% after 30min
(Walberer et al., 2012). With respect to the consistency of vessel occlusion, our data support the macrosphere model being a
coequal alternative to the suture model with permanent occlusion
(Fig. 7).
In our study, fpVCT investigation of CBV 24h after occlusion
failedtodetectsignificantdifferencesneitherbetweencontrolsand
interventional groups, nor within different stroke models (Fig. 8).
McLeod et al. demonstrated feasibility and validity of a rat CTPerfusion stroke model during the acute phase of three hours after
MCAO using a 64 slice CT-scanner. Compared to CBF, they found
CBV maps to be less clear-cut, concluding that CBF is a better
predictor of infarction (McLeod et al., 2011). These findings correspond to observations from clinical research 3–6h after symptom
onset where comparison of CT thresholds for cerebral infarction
and diffusion-weighted MRI showed CBF thresholds on perfusion
CT to define the acute infarct core more accurately than other CT
thresholds (Bivard et al., 2011).
Flat-panel based computed tomography has proven its beneficial capacity in visualizations of fine details in high-contrast
situations of bone imaging (Obert et al., 2005; Schulte-Geers et al.,
2011).Ontheotherhand,theimagequalitywithrespecttocontrast
and resolution is rather poor in low-contrast imaging conditions as
they are given in brain parenchyma imaging. Figs. 3a and b and 4
demonstrate this, as there are no differences between white and
gray matter visible, nor are any ventricular structures detectable.
Further limitations of our study may include the number of animals per group as a cause for lacking statistical significance in
places. We used healthy animals under strictly controlled laboratory conditions in contrast to normally multimorbid stroke
patients. Susceptibility differences to ischemia between species
have to be taken into account when findings are compared. Methods used in this experiment had to be applied under anesthesia
that may have had an influence on perfusion parameters. Beyond
that, cerebral energy metabolism and blood flow in mammals are
inversely related to bodyweight: Thus in rats, glucose and oxygen
metabolism as well as cerebral blood flow are three times as high
as in humans. In addition, there seem to be more effective collaterals between large cerebral vessels in rats and spatial as well as
temporal progress of ischemia may differ (Dirnagl et al., 1999).
5. Conclusion
In summary, we could demonstrate feasibility of cerebral perfusion measurement in rats with the fpVCT, being able to detect
hypoperfusion by decreased cerebral blood flow 24h after middle
cerebral artery occlusion. Perfusion data confirm the macrosphere
model as a useful alternative for permanent vessel occlusion in
experimental stroke. We assume that fpVCT can be a useful tool for
non-invasivedynamicimagingofcerebralperfusioninrodentmodels for stroke and may help facilitating studies on pathophysiology
and therapy in future.
