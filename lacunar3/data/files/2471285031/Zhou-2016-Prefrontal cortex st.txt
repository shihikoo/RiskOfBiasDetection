Prefrontal cortex stroke induces delayed impairment in spatial
memory
Lisa Y.Y. Zhoua, Tim E. Wrighta, Andrew N. Clarksona,b,c,∗
a Department of Anatomy, Brain Health Research Center and Brain Research new Zealand, University of Otago, P.O. Box 913, Dunedin 9054, New Zealand

h i g h l i g h t s
• Stroke to the PFC of mice does not affect motor function or anxiety.
• Stroke to the PFC of mice does not affect novel object recognition.
• Stroke to the PFC of mice results in delayed onset impairment in spatial memory.
• Stroke to the PFC of mice results in decreased volume of the medial dorsal thalamic nuclei.
• We describe here a model of stroke that can be used to assess the underlying causes of delayed impairments in cognition.

Strokeistheleadingcauseoflong-termdisability.Littleisknownabouttheeffectsofstrokeoncognitive
deficits. The subtle nature of cognition and its respective domains in areas such as working memory and
attention can make this difficult to diagnose and treat. We aimed to establish a model of focal ischemia
that targets the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and induce memory impairments. Stroke and sham mice were
assessedatoneandfour-weekspost-strokeonvarioustests:open-fieldtasktoassessactivity;grid-walk
and cylinder task to assess motor impairments; elevated plus maze to assess anxiety; novel-object and
object-location recognition tasks to assess memory impairment. Stroke mice in the open-field showed a
smallincreaseinactivitywithnoeffectsongrossmotortasksoranxietylevels(P≥0.05)atoneandfourweeks post-stroke. Assessment of stroke mice on the novel object task showed no differences at either
oneorfour-weekscomparedtoshammice(P≥0.05).However,assessmentofstrokemiceontheobjectlocation recognition task revealed a significant (P≥0.05) impairment in spatial memory by four-weeks
compared to controls. Further, we show that stroke results in a small decrease in volume of the medial
dorsal nucleus of the thalamus (P≥0.05). This is the first evidence that demonstrates stroke to the PFC
resultsindelayedonsetimpairmentinspatialmemory,similartofindingsinhumanepidemiologicaldata.
We suggest that this model may be a useful tool in assessing potential rehabilitative/cognitive therapies
after stroke.

Small strokes to the medial PFC are often associated with
impaired executive function and memory, with the impairment
developing progressively after the insult. Therefore, establishing
animalmodelsofstrokethatallowsfortheassessmentofcognitive
and memory deficits is essential to finding underlying causes and
developing potential therapies. To further address these issues, a
recent study by Cordova et al. established a rat medial PFC stroke
model that shows deficits in executive function [9]. More specifically this dysfunction influenced selective deficits in attention
during an extra-dimensional set-shifting task [9]. We report here
thatstroketothePFCinmiceresultsindelayedonsetimpairmentin
spatialmemory.Importantlythisdelayedonsetimpairmentissimilar to what is observed in human stroke patients where memory
is affected [5,10].
Spatial memory is preferentially thought to be a hippocampaldependent process, but relies on intact neuronal networks
communicating between several brain regions; such as cingulum
bundle, retrosplenial cortex, prefrontal cortex, and thalamus; and
breakdown in these pathways can result in impaired spatial memory [11–15]. To date, lesions to the PFC have resulted in mixed
findings related to impaired spatial memory. Experimental findings from Ennaceur et al. report no impairment in spatial memory
on the OLR task when assessed 4-months post-lesion [13]. In spite
of this finding, the same study found significant impairments in
location recognition memory when lesions were applied to either
thefornixorcingulumbundle[13].Thisfindingisofinterestasthe
cingulum bundle links the hippocampus, retrosplenial cortex, and
prefrontal cortex [14], regions all known to be involved with spatial memory [11]. In agreement with our findings, mice that have
had a PFC lesion show impairment in the location component of
a what-when-where variant of the object recognition task, but no
impairment in either the recognition or temporal order components[15].Itispossiblethatbyfourmonthsthebrainsofmicehave
undergoneremodelingandhavecompensatedfortheimpairment
we and others report at earlier time points.
A likely explanation for the delayed onset impairment in spatial memory is the progressive loss of connections between brain
regions. Understanding how these connections are lost over time
will be critical in finding therapies that can: (1) prevent the loss
of connection or (2) enhance the re-organisation of new connections and facilitate functional recovery. This re-organization of
connections throughout the brain has been previously reported
to be associated with the re-mapping of both local and longdistanceconnectionslinkedtoregionsofinjury[3,6,16,17],andthe
re-mapping of lost functions correlated with functional recovery
[6,17,18].
A likely consequence of the loss in connectivity between brain
regionsissecondaryneurodegeneration/diaschisis,whichiswhere
cell loss occurs in remote, yet functionally connected brain regions
to the site of injury [19,20]. This has been previously reported to
occurinbothanimalmodelsofstrokeandinhumanstrokepatients
