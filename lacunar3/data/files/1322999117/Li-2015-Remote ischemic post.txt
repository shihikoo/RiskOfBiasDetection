by AQP4 down-regulation in astrocytes

h i g h l i g h t s
• We make the middle cerebral artery occlusion (MACO) model and remote ischemic post-conditioning (RIPC) model.
• We examine changes in the level of AQP4 for each model.
• RIPC suppresses the ischemia-induced reduction of AQP4 expression.
• The neuroprotective mechanism of RIPC is mediated by the down-regulation of AQP4.
• RIPC can serve as complementary and alternative treatments to protect brain from ischemic stroke.

a b s t r a c t
Cerebral ischemia is a primary cause of human death and long-term disability. Previous studies have
suggested that remote ischemic post-conditioning (RIPC) is a potential useful tool for cerebral ischemic
treatment.However,theprotectivemechanismofRIPCisnotveryclear.Thisstudyverifiedthehypothesis
that,inremotepost-conditioningofcerebralischemicrats,down-regulationofaquaporin4(AQP4),which
is an important player for water hemostasis in astrocytes, could attenuate cerebral damage after transient
middle cerebral artery occlusion (MCAO). In this study, RIPC model was established after MCAO. Each hind
limb of rat was clamped by small rubber tubes for 10min, and then the tubes were opened for 10min.
The clamping and opening were operated for a total of three cycles to block the hind limbs blood flow.
The results showed that, RIPC could significantly improve neurological function, decrease the percentage
of the infarct volume and edema, and elevate the integrity of blood–brain barrier (BBB). In addition, the
numbers of AQP4 and glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) positive cells were significantly lower in the
RIPC group. Moreover, we found that AQP4 expression decreased in response to ischemia/reperfusion
in the RIPC group. Our findings indicated that RIPC could attenuate focal cerebral ischemia/reperfusion
injury, and the neuroprotective mechanism was related with the down-regulation of AQP4 in astrocytes.

1. Introduction
Ischemic stroke is an emergency setting. It is the third leading
cause of death and the first leading cause of long-term disability
in developed countries [1]. The pathological process of ischemic
stroke is influenced by many factors. Numerous researchers have
suggested that edema and blood–brain barrier (BBB) disruption
could result in brain tissue injury after ischemic stroke [2,3].
Aquaporins (AQPs) play essential roles in the pathogenesis of
brain edema. As the members of the water channel protein family,
they are highly expressed in brain tissue, especially in astrocytic
end-feet. In the central nervous system, water channel protein
mainly expressed in astrocytic feet, BBB, subpial and subependymal regions. AQP4 is a water channel protein localized in the end
foot of astrocytes in the brain, and its expression is up-regulated
after a stab wound to the mouse brain [4]. It is an important player
for water hemostasis during the development of cytotoxic as well
as vasogenic edema. Increased expression of AQPs is important in
pathophysiology of neurological diseases such as neural inflammation and ischemia [5]. It has been known that, AQP4 could be linked
to permeabilization of the BBB in cerebral ischemia/reperfusion
injury (CI/RI) [6]. Many literatures stated that AQP4 plays a pivotal role in ischemia-induced brain edema [7]. Moreover, AQP4
facilitates water movement into injured astrocytes across the BBB.
Previous studies showed reduced cytotoxic brain edema with
improved neurological outcome encountered in AQP4 knockout
mice after cerebral ischemia [8]. Cerebral edema resulting from
AQP4 over-expression is considered to be one of the major determinants for progressive neuronal insult during cerebral ischemia.
In a word, over expression of AQP4 is one of the chief contributors
of water imbalance in brain during ischemic pathology [9]. However
, in ischemic brain, the role of AQP4 in intervention of RIPC is
unknown.
Ischemic post-conditioning, which refers to a series of brief
ischemia and reperfusion cycles, applied immediately at the site
of the ischemic organ. It can result in reduced infarction in both
cerebral and myocardial ischemia [10]. Post-conditioning can be
mimicked using anesthetics or other pharmacological agents as
stimulitoprotectagainstischemia/reperfusioninjuryorperformed
in a distant organ [11]. RIPC, one of ischemic post-conditioning, is a
promising therapeutic intervention in which a sub-lethal ischemic
insult was induced in one organ (limb) to improve ischemia in an
organ distant to it (brain) [12]. Some studies showed that RIPC
could protect the ischemic brain via signaling pathways and factors
[13]. However, the change of AQP4 in RIPC is kept to be elusive. As
RIPC has protective effects on the brain, we proposed that AQP4
expression may be decreased in the distal reperfusion condition.
In this study, we tested the neuroprotective effect of limb RIPC
using the transient focal ischemic stroke model and investigated
the potential protective mechanisms related to AQP4.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Animals and grouping
Adult female Sprague–Dawley rats (weighing 250–280g, 7
weeks) were obtained from Chengdu DaShuo Biotechnological
Company (Chengdu, China). Rats were housed in plastic cages with
soft bedding and free access to food and water in an environmentally controlled room (at 22–26 ◦C, 56–70% humidity, with a 12/12h
light/dark cycle). Protocols of the animal approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at Chengdu Medical College
were used. All animals were treated according to the principles
stated in relevant Chinese stipulations on animal experiments and
in conformity with the Guide for Stroke Therapy Academic Industry Roundtable (STAIR) criteria. The rats did not have hypertensive
and diabetic diseases.
All rats were allocated in 20 cages. Animals were randomly allocated to the following experimental groups: sham group, model
group and RIPC group. 1–2 rats from each cage were randomly
picked for sham processing, 41 rats totally. 74 and 70 rats were randomly selected from the remaining for MACO and RIPC processing,
respectively. The number of rats in each group represented the
actual usage suitable for the statistical analyses, and did not include
the rats which were eventually eliminated. Every group was further
divided into two sub-groups: 1 and 3 days. There was no significant
difference between the sham sub-groups.
2.2. Rat model establishment
Transient MCAO was performed using the intraluminal filament
method as described previously [14] with slight modifications.
In all groups, rats were weighed and anesthetized by intraperitoneal injection of 10% chloral hydrate mixed solution containing
0.9mg/100mL of sodium chloride and 10mg/100mL chloral
hydrate (350mg/kg, IP) before surgery. Except the sham group, the
cerebral ischemia/reperfusion model was established in rats from
the MCAO and RIPC group, via occlusion of the middle cerebral
artery. In brief, a midline incision was cut along the neck, followed
by separation of the right common carotid artery, internal carotid
artery and external carotid artery. A 0.26mm nylon filament with
a blunt tip was inserted into the common carotid artery, and softly
advanced 18–20mm from the branch point of the carotid artery
along the internal carotid artery until mild resistance was felt. One

restoration, the wound was sutured and the animals were allowed

RIPC intervention: During reperfusion, rats were randomly

was induced by encircling this snare around each hind limb of the
rat. The bilateral femoral arteries was blocked for 10min and then
opened for 10min for a total of three cycles. During the blocking
process, pulse sensors placed on the bilateral dorsalis pedis arteries detected the pulses to determine whether the blood flow in
the limbs was blocked. Limb ischemia was confirmed by the disappearance of pulse, limbs expansion and cyanosis. In addition,
Reperfusion was confirmed by the limbs return to their original
pink color. Following the RIPC, the rats’ normal activities including
walking, feeding and sleeping were closely observed. In the sham
and MCAO groups, there is no related processing.
2.3. Assessment of neurological deficits
All tests were performed after 1 and 3 days of reperfusion. Neurologicalbehaviorofratswasassessedbythreeobserverswhowere
blinded to the experimental conditions according to the methods of Longa et al. [15]. Finally, the scores were averaged. Score
0: normal condition with no neurological sign; Score 1: the left
anterior paw couldn’t completely stretch; Score 2: rats rotated
to the left side; Score 3: rats slumped to the left when walking;
Score 4: rats couldn’t walk independently and lose consciousness.
Animals which died from pulmonary insufficiency and suffocation
were excluded from the study [16]. The higher the score got, the
higher mortality had. In order to damage the brain as much as
possible, the 1–2 score animals were brought into the following
experimentalprocedure:shamgroup,MCAOgroupandRIPCgroup.
Due to the Garcia JH [17] particularly evaluated the motor function
and sensory function of rats with different score, which made the
scoremeaningful.TheneurologicalscoringmethodofGarciaJHwas
used to assess the nerve function damage degree of the rats from
six aspects (voluntary movement, body symmetry, arms stretched
function, climbing movement, body touch sensation on both sides,
bilateral beard reflection). The score of Garcia JH is between 3 and
18. The lighter the neurologic injury was, the larger the score would
get. Rats with score 18 was normal.
2.4. Tissue collection and making slices
Different material processing should be selected for requirements for different experimental methods. Following the deeply
anesthesia, the animal brains for histological staining were firstly
cleaned rapidly with 0.9% NaCl via heart perfusion which was
performed at days 1 and 3 and slowly pre-fixed in 10% buffered formalin. Then the skull was opened and the brain removed quickly.
Therewasnoanyprocessingafteranesthesia,tissuesobtainedfrom
right middle cerebral artery territory of the hemisphere were used
for PCR analysis and placed in a freezer at −80 ◦C. Furthermore,
if there was evidence of subarachnoid hemorrhage in extraction of
brain tissue, the animal should be excluded from the study. A 4mm
coronal brain slices was achieved from the area 5mm behind the
frontal pole and post-fixed in 10% buffered formalin for 24h. Then
the slices were embedded in paraffin and further sliced into 5m
thickness sections. These slices were used for histological investigation. These methods of evaluation were performed by a blinded
pathologist.
2.5. Measurement of infarct volumes and brain edema
After assessment of neurological function, rats (sham group:
n=5, MCAO group: n=16, RIPC group: n=16) were narcotized by
intraperitoneal injection with 10% chloral hydrate (100g/0.3mL)
and sacrificed by decapitation at days 1 and 3 after reperfusion. Brains were quickly removed and chilled at −20 ◦C for
10min. Then six consecutive 2-mm coronal sections were prepared
, beginning from the anterior pole. The sections were
immediately immersed in 0.1% 2,3,5-triphenyltetrazolium chloride (TTC; Sigma, USA) prepared in phosphate buffered saline
(PBS) for 30min at 37 ◦C and then fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 1h. The infarcted tissue remained unstained (white),
whereas normal tissue was stained as red. The sections were
photographed using a digital camera (Olympus, Japan). The infarct
zone was demarcated and analyzed by Image J software. In brief,
the ratio of infarct to normal volume was calculated according
to the following formula [18]: infarct volume %=[(contralateral
hemisphere volume−non-infarcted region in the ipsilateral hemisphere
)/contralateral hemisphere volume]×100%. At the same
time, for the evaluation of brain edema according to the results
of TTC, the ipsilateral and contralateral hemispheres were used
to calculate the percentage of brain edema [19]: brain edema
%=[(volume of ipsilateral hemisphere−volume of contralateral
hemisphere volume)/contralateral hemisphere volume]×100%.
2.6. Evaluation of blood–brain barrier permeability
The permeability of BBB was assessed by the leakage of Evans
blue (EB) following its intravenous injection. That is to say, 2%
Evans Blue tracer (4mL/kg) (Sigma Aldrich, Germany) diluted in
0.9% NaCl was intravenously injected into tail vein at 1h before
the animal was sacrificed, then the rats (sham group: n=10, MCAO
group:n=18, RIPC group:n=14) were transcardially perfused with
saline to clear the remaining dye in the blood vessels. The brain
was quickly removed, weighed and cut up into fine particles and
immersed in methanamide for 24h at 60 ◦C. The fine particles were
centrifugated at 12,000×g for 20min, and then the absorption of
the supernatant was measured at 635nm with a spectrophotometer (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). The EB content was quantified as
micrograms of EB per gram of tissue using a standardized curve,
with methanamide as negative control.

2.8. RT-PCR analysis
Expression levels of AQP4 and GFAP mRNA were determined
by Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR).

were used for RT-PCR. Total RNA was extracted from the lesion
boundary zone of brain using an RNeasy Mini Kit (Omega Bio-Tek,
Norcross, GA, USA). Extracted RNA was then reverse transcribed

CA, USA) with the PlexorTM One-Step qRT-PCR System (Promega,
Madison, WI, USA). The cDNA was used as the template in

six aspects: voluntary movement, body symmetry, arms stretched
function, climbing movement, body touch sensation on both sides,
bilateral beard reflection. Compared with sham group, decreasing
Garcia JH score showed that the animals had decreasing neurological function (P<0.01). However, after RIPC, the RIPC group score
was significantly higher than that of MCAO group at each time point
(P<0.05, Fig. 1A). So we concluded that neurological function was
improved after RIPC. In order to detect the underlying mechanism
of neurology improvement after RIPC, we measured brain edema
at reperfusion 1 and 3 days after MCAO injury using TTC staining.
White areas in brain are damaged due to ischemia (Fig. 1B). Based
on the result of TTC, we found that all RIPC animals have less percentage of cerebral infarction volume than that of MCAO group and
it showed a statistic significance at days 1 and 3 (P<0.05, Fig. 1C).
Furthermore, brain edema was significantly reduced in RIPC group
at days 1 and 3 (P<0.05, Fig. 1D) compared with the MCAO group.
That is to say, RIPC could alleviate brain edema and reduce the
infarct volume from cerebra ischemia.
3.2. RIPC preserved BBB integrity
EB leakage was detected to evaluate the effect of RIPC on the
integrity of the BBB following cerebral ischemia reperfusion. The
results showed that, in MCAO and RIPC groups, the leakages of
EB were significantly different from that of sham group at days
1 and 3 (Fig. 2B). Rats treated with RIPC exhibited a smaller and
less significant amount of EB extravasations (Fig. 2A). These data
demonstrated that RIPC remarkably alleviated the impairment of
the BBB induced by brain ischemia.
3.3. Expression of GFAP in the brain
In this study, we found that RIPC could decrease the expression
of GFAP. The number of GFAP positive cells in the ischemic cortex
was tested by immunohistochemistry analysis. The expression of
GFAP was marked with yellow staining in cytoplasm of astrocytes
in the brain. In the MCAO and RIPC groups, GFAP immunopositive profiles in cortex were stronger than that of the sham group
(P<0.01, Fig. 3B). In addition, the number of GFAP positive cells in
RIPC group was significantly lower than that in MCAO group at days
1 and 3 (P<0.05, Fig. 3B), which indicated that RIPC might inhibit
the express of GFAP in the cortex. To further explore the change
of GFAP mRNA expression, the RT-PCR results showed the mRNA
expression of GFAP was significantly increased in MCAO group
comparing to sham group, and RIPC decreased mRNA expression
relative in MCAO group at day 1 (P<0.01, Fig. 3C). However there
was no statistical significance at day 3 (P>0.05). These demonstrated that the change of GFAP expression in the cerebral ischemic
was significantly higher and RIPC was effective for inhibiting GFAP
expression.

Double immunofluorescence staining was used to detect the
localization of AQP4 in brain. AQP4 and GFAP immune labeling in the ischemic brain showed a related co-localization. The
immunoreactivityofAQP4couldbeseeninthevascularendothelial
cells,aswellasGFAP+ astrocytesaroundthevessels.Co-localization
of AQP4 and GFAP exhibited in brain after ischemic injury (Fig. 4).

4. Discussion
The present study showed that RIPC performed by three cycles
of 10-min ischemia and 10-min reperfusion in the hind limbs, could
decrease the cerebral infarct, edema and BBB disruption, moreover
, improve the neurofunctional recovery from ischemic stroke
via down-regulating the expression of AQP4 in astrocytes in rats.
It has been widely proved that RIPC could protect the brain
from ischemic stroke [12,13]. However, the mechanism of RIPC
protection remained unclear. Recently, some papers showed that
the neuroprotective mechanism of RIPC was mediated by an antiapoptotic effect and reduction of the inflammatory response and
alleviated cerebral ischemic injury by attenuating endoplasmic
reticulumstress-mediatedapoptosis[20].Inaddition,non-invasive
remote limb ischemic post-conditioning protected rats against
focal cerebral ischemia by the up-regulation of STAT3 and

To estimate the degree of brain damage accounting for the

AQP4 might be involved in change of astrocyte and astrocyterelated behaviors during aging [27]. A secondary and often lethal
consequence of traumatic brain injury was cellular edema, so
astrocytic swelling was caused by transmembrane water fluxes
augmented by vasopressin-regulated AQP4 [28]. During the last
few years, a number of studies had shown that astrocytes swelled
in the presence of cerebral ischemia. As is known to all, during
the early hours of cerebral ischemia, edema was formed by a process involving osmotically obliged water across BBB from the blood
into the brain. At the same time, perivascular astrocytes rapidly
swelled as they took up water to enter the brain, forming cytotoxic
edema. A number of studies had provided evidence that BBB disruption

was previously reported to increase AQP4 expression in the infarct
zone shortly after the insult [31]. On the other hand, another study
suggested that AQP4 inhibition might provide a new therapeutic option for reducing brain edema [32]. AQP4 was associated
with brain damage after MCAO. Numerous studies demonstrated
that cerebral edema may be attenuated via the regulation of AQP4
expression, thus initiating a novel therapeutic strategy against this
possibly fatal condition [33].
Using TTC staining, our findings indicated that RIPC could significantly reduce the cerebral infarct volume and edema induced
by cerebral ischemia, and attenuate the expression of AQP4 in
the ischemic brain, which suggested that the protective mechanism of RIPC was partially dependent on the down-regulation
of AQP4 expression contributing to the improvement of functional outcomes. Our data showed increasing expression of AQP4 in
MCAO group, while RIPC significantly down-regulated AQP4 mRNA
levels after MCAO especially at day 1, which suggested that downregulated of AQP4 could improve brain damage at early time of
cerebral ischemia.
